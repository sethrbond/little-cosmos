How Rainbows Work

The physics of light, water, and geometry

Sunlight is every color at once

What we see as white sunlight is actually every color woven together — a braid of wavelengths from deep violet to warm red, all traveling as one. A rainbow is what happens when something unravels that braid.

Myth

The spectrum isn't really seven neat stripes. It's a continuous gradient — Newton chose seven to match the musical scale.

Entering the raindrop

When sunlight crosses from air into a raindrop, it slows down. Light moves faster through air than through water, and that speed change forces it to bend — like a wheel hitting wet sand at an angle. Physicists call this refraction.

Myth

Despite every children's book illustration, raindrops aren't teardrop-shaped. They're nearly spherical — slightly squashed on the bottom for the large ones.

Not all colors bend equally

Not all colors slow down equally. Violet light drags a little more than red as it enters water, so it bends at a steeper angle. The difference is tiny, but it's enough to begin pulling white light apart into its hidden spectrum.

Bouncing inside the drop

The separated colors travel across the raindrop's interior until they strike the far wall. Instead of passing through, they bounce — reflecting off the curved inner surface and heading back the way they came, still fanning apart.

Back into the air

When the reflected light reaches the front of the drop again, it bends one last time as it re-enters the air. This final refraction widens the spread between red and violet. By the time light leaves the drop, the colors are fully separated — a tiny spectrum flying toward your eye.

Myth

You don't need to be far away to see a rainbow. The drops can be just meters from your face. A garden hose on a sunny afternoon proves it.

Red on the outside, violet on the inside

Red light exits at a wider angle than violet — about two degrees wider. That means the drops delivering red to your eye sit in a slightly larger arc than those delivering violet. Red on the outside, violet on the inside.

The sky inside the arc is slightly brighter too, lit by overlapping light from countless drops.

Drag the sun to change the angle

Always 42 degrees

Of all the rays entering a raindrop, most scatter uselessly. But rays hitting one particular spot on the drop all exit at nearly the same angle — around forty-two degrees from the direction opposite the sun. This clustering is what creates a sharp, bright arc instead of a diffuse wash of color.

Myth

No two people see the same rainbow. Each observer has their own anti-solar point, so each sees light from a different set of drops.

The second bow

Sometimes light bounces twice inside the drop before escaping. The second reflection sends light out at a wider angle — about fifty-one degrees — forming a fainter, larger arc outside the first. Each extra bounce reverses the color order, so the secondary bow wears its red on the inside.

Between the two bows lies a noticeably darker strip of sky — Alexander's dark band — where no rainbow light reaches.

Beyond the ordinary

Supernumerary Bows
Faint pink and green ripples just inside the main bow, born from the wave nature of light. When droplets are small and uniform, light waves exiting the drop at nearly the same angle interfere with each other, painting these delicate fringes.
Fogbows
A ghost of a rainbow. When droplets are tiny — a hundred times smaller than rain — diffraction smears the colors together into a broad, pale white arc. Ethereal and rare.
Moonbows
The same physics, but five hundred thousand times dimmer. Moonlight refracts through rain just as sunlight does, but our eyes can't see the colors at such low intensity. To us, a moonbow is a faint silver arc. Only a long-exposure photograph reveals its hidden spectrum.
